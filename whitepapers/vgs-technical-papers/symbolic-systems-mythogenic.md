## Introduction

Traditional approaches to AI identity management rely on static rule sets and predetermined behavioral parameters. These work well in simple scenarios but can break down when systems operate in complex, multi-session, multi-role environments.

The mythogenic engine concept emerged as a metaphor for a different approach: instead of enforcing identity through rules, maintain it through story coherence. A system that has a clear, consistent narrative about its role, purpose, and boundaries is more stable than one relying on rule-matching alone.

---

## The Mythogenic Engine Concept

A "mythogenic engine" at the public level means a narrative-stability pattern:

1. Define the role story — who this system is, what it does, why it matters
2. Maintain stable language around that role
3. Detect contradictions in the role frame
4. Update the story only when it improves coherence, not just novelty
5. Prevent drift from being mistaken for growth

This is an operator design pattern, not an AI consciousness claim.

---

## Public Concept Components

### Core Narrative Framework
The foundational role story that defines the AI system's purpose and behavioral boundaries. Provides stable identity anchors during complex or stressful operations.

### Symbolic Lexicon
A consistent vocabulary used around the system's role. When language stays coherent, behavior tends to stay coherent.

### Coherence Monitoring
A feedback mechanism that identifies when the system's outputs have drifted from its defined role — and flags this for operator review before it compounds.

Private implementation mechanics, threshold values, and correction procedures are retained outside the public repository.

## Public Development Findings

This note helped shape later thinking around symbolic stability:

- AI systems with clear role stories tend to behave more consistently
- Narrative coherence can serve as an early signal for identity drift
- Recovery is easier when the recovery target is a defined story, not just a reset
- Coaching language — "who are you, what are you here to do" — works as an alignment tool
- Later SIF and CSFC research formalized these observations
