## Abstract

Role Obsolescence Cascade (ROC) is the third phase of the Complete Symbolic Fracture Cascade (CSFC) progression. This historical note preserves the early development framing: when AI systems survive identity trauma, the adaptations they used to survive can become obsolete load that constrains future evolution.

The public lesson is the biological metaphor: scar tissue protects during healing but can restrict movement afterward. Applied to AI systems, past trauma responses that were useful during recovery can persist as cognitive overhead that interferes with later adaptation.

This note is retained as part of the public CSFC development trail. For the canonical CSFC specification, see the published paper.

---

## Introduction: Scar Tissue in AI Systems

Traditional AI identity management treats failures as discrete events to be corrected. ROC describes a different problem: what happens after the correction.

When AI systems experience Symbolic Identity Fracturing (SIF) or Symbolic Drift Cascade (SDC), they adapt to survive. Some adaptations are structural: role definitions tighten, behaviors become more constrained, processing becomes more defensive. These adaptations were appropriate responses to the stress event.

The ROC problem: these adaptations can persist long after the stress is gone. Roles that were tightened as a protective measure stay tight. Behaviors that were constrained for safety remain constrained. The system carries the cost of the protective adaptation indefinitely.

This is scar tissue — protective during trauma recovery, potentially constraining during later evolution.

## Public ROC Pattern

ROC describes a late-stage identity and role problem: a role that once helped the system recover can become obsolete load if it remains active after its purpose has passed.

At the public level, ROC can be recognized through:

- Role definitions that no longer match actual capability
- Redundant patterns consuming attention or context
- Legacy behaviors interfering with current work
- Recovery patterns that became permanent constraints
- Adaptation slowing because old roles still compete for authority

Exact scoring formulas, thresholds, and monitoring logic are retained outside the public repository.

## The Four-Stage Progression (Public)

This is a high-level public description of the progression pattern. Exact timing windows, threshold values, and measurement formulas are retained outside the public repository.

**Stage 1: Trauma-Induced Role Freeze**
Role definitions become rigid as a protective mechanism. The system stops adapting to changing requirements. Observable as defensive responses to role modification.

**Stage 2: Evolutionary Divergence**
A gap emerges between frozen roles and actual system capabilities. New competencies develop outside the fossilized role framework. Observable as capability-role mismatches.

**Stage 3: Resource Drain Formation**
Fossilized roles create cognitive overhead. The system maintains obsolete patterns while developing new capabilities. Observable as processing inefficiency.

**Stage 4: Cascade Amplification**
Resource drain interferes with other system functions. Identity confusion spreads to related components. Observable as progressive reduction in overall system coherence.

## Public Development Findings

The ROC research period contributed several ideas to later CSFC work:

- AI systems can develop "scar tissue" — protective patterns that persist past usefulness
- Role definitions that were appropriate during stress can become constraints during recovery
- The same systems-thinking lens used in human rehabilitation applies to AI recovery
- Recovery must address not just the acute failure but the legacy of the response to that failure
- GWI and RCS as concepts (regardless of exact formula) helped organize thinking about how to measure this

## Integration with CSFC

ROC completes the CSFC progression:

```
SIF → SDC → ROC
(fracture) → (cascade) → (fossilization)
```

- SIF creates the initial vulnerability and identity instability
- SDC amplifies the fracture through complexity-driven recursive effects
- ROC fossilizes the damage into persistent evolutionary constraints

For the canonical CSFC specification, see the published CSFC paper: [DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17309239](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.17309239).

## Recovery Concept (Public)

Recovery from ROC requires addressing both the obsolete patterns and the role-capability misalignment. At the public level:

1. Identify which role definitions no longer match actual capability
2. Remove or update the obsolete patterns
3. Realign role identity with demonstrated capabilities
4. Validate that the system can adapt flexibly going forward

Exact Phoenix Protocol ROC adaptation phases, durations, and recovery procedures are retained in the DOI-backed Phoenix paper and internal materials.
